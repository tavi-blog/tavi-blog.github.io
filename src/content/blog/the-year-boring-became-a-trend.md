title: "\U0001F4AA The Year Boring Became a Trend"
pubDate: '2026-07-11T20:30:37-04:00'
---
The major fitness industry bodies put out their trend forecasts for the year, and buried under the usual list of wearables and recovery gadgets was something almost funny: sustainability is in, crash transformations are out. Clients, the surveys say, are no longer chasing a look by a deadline. They want something they can keep doing. Evidence-backed, personalized, built for longevity instead of a before-and-after photo. The industry is calling this a trend. I've been calling it consistency for two years.

For most of the time I've spent training, the only two modes anyone was selling were bulk and cut, and everything in between got treated like an unsolved mystery. Eat in a surplus, get big, accept the fat that comes with it, then eat in a deficit and burn it back off, and repeat the cycle like a pendulum, because a pendulum makes for a better before-and-after post than something that barely changes shape from one photo to the next. The extremes have a real appeal, and it's worth admitting why before dismissing them: they're legible. A surplus and a deficit each tell a story with a clear beginning and a clear payoff. Recomposition, eating close to maintenance while training and prioritizing protein, produces a body that changes slowly enough that even the person living in it has trouble seeing the difference month to month. It's much harder to sell a photo of nothing dramatic happening.

The same reports note that longevity has overtaken appearance as the fastest-growing goal clients bring to a gym, ahead of the wearables and recovery tools that usually dominate these lists. That's a real shift in what people say they want, and it lines up with something I noticed well before any survey said it back to me: once the goal stops being a deadline and starts being a decade, the pendulum swing between surplus and deficit stops making sense as a strategy. You can't crash-diet your way into a decade. You can only show up for it.

That's the part the new trend reports don't quite say out loud, which is that the boring approach was never a secret. It's just unmarketable, so nobody with a program to sell had much incentive to lead with it. I dropped a significant amount of weight once through pure restriction, no training involved, and what I got for it was a smaller version of the same soft body, because calories without a training stimulus just shrink you, they don't reshape you. Rebuilding from there meant giving up the dopamine of a scale number moving fast and accepting a much slower, much less photogenic process: a push-pull-legs split, protein as the one number I actually track closely, everything else held loosely enough that I can keep doing it indefinitely instead of gutting it out for eight weeks and quitting.

What's changed isn't the practice. It's that the industry has finally run out of new extremes to sell and had to package the boring thing as the next one. That's a genuinely useful shift for anyone who was about to start a crash program based on last year's advice, and I don't want to undersell that. A public health message that says slow and sustainable beats fast and unsustainable is a better message than the one it's replacing, even if the reason it's being said out loud now has more to do with market saturation than any change of heart.

I do wonder what gets lost when consistency becomes a product category instead of just a habit. The version of this that gets sold back to people usually comes with a coach, a personalized plan, an app subscription, a longevity assessment, because an industry built on selling things can't easily sell "eat roughly the same amount every day, lift consistently, and give it a year." That advice doesn't need a subscription. It needs patience, which is the one thing that's never been for sale, no matter what year's trend report says otherwise. The people with the least slack in their schedule and their budget are the ones least able to afford the personalized version of an approach that used to just require a scale, a barbell, and the willingness to look the same in the mirror for months at a time. Somebody working two jobs with no time to spare for a coaching call doesn't need the trend report to validate consistency. They needed it to be sellable as free advice a decade ago, and mostly it wasn't, because free advice doesn't fund an industry the size of this one.

I don't think there's anything wrong with the shift in messaging, and I'd rather live in a year where "sustainable" is the buzzword than one where the buzzword is a sixteen-week transformation. But I notice the whiplash of watching something I did out of necessity, because the fast version already failed me once, get repackaged as this year's insight. It's a strange thing to watch your own default get validated by an industry that spent years selling you the opposite of it, and stranger still to know the validation arrived not because anyone changed their mind about what works, but because the old pitch stopped converting. The body recomposition itself doesn't care what the trend report calls it. It just keeps requiring the same unglamorous inputs it always has, on a timeline long enough that nobody's going to build a marketing campaign around it, no matter how many times the industry rediscovers that boring works.
